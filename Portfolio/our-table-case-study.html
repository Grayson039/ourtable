01 — The Problem

Recipe apps are built for individuals.
Households cook together.

The average household cook manages 2–4 people with different dietary needs, saves recipes constantly from social media, and shops weekly without knowing what's already in the fridge. Every existing app solves one of these problems. None solve all three.

Problem 01

The saving problem

TikTok recipe. Instagram reel. Random food blog. Screenshotted and lost. No app makes it frictionless to import from every surface — so recipes stay in camera rolls, never cooked.

Problem 02

The fridge problem

"What can I make with what we have?" is answered by opening the fridge and guessing. The grocery list and the pantry are never connected. Food expires. You shop for things you already have.

Problem 03

The household problem

One person is dairy-free. One is vegetarian. The app doesn't know. Recipes get bookmarked that can't actually be made for everyone. There's no shared list, no shared library, no shared context.

The gap: Paprika, Yummly, and Whisk each solve a slice. None of them combine social import + fridge intelligence + household-aware cooking in a single product. That's the gap Our Table fills.

02 — The Solution

Three differentiators.
One product.

Our Table is the first recipe app designed for the household as the primary unit — not the individual. The three core differentiators each independently justify switching from any existing app. Together they create something with no direct competitor.

Differentiator 01
📱

Social-first import

Paste any TikTok, Instagram, or food blog URL. Recipe is extracted, cleaned, and saved in under 5 seconds. No manual entry. Works on every platform.

Differentiator 02
📸

Fridge scanner

Snap your fridge. The app detects ingredients and surfaces recipes you can make right now from what you already have. Reduces food waste, eliminates the "what's for dinner?" paralysis.

Differentiator 03
👨‍👩‍👧

Household-aware

Every member's dietary restrictions are tracked. Recipes auto-flag conflicts. The grocery list is shared. Ingredients scale for your household size. Built for a table of 4, not a user of 1.

03 — Process

How we got here

Phase 1 — Ideation

Gap identified, competitors mapped

Surveyed Paprika, Yummly, and Whisk. Found the universal gap: no app combined social import + fridge intelligence + household context. Defined the North Star user: the household cook managing 2–4 people with mixed dietary needs who saves recipes from social media daily.

Phase 2 — UX Architecture

Navigation and information architecture locked

Chose a 5-tab navigation: Home, Search, Fridge, List, Me. The Fridge scanner was elevated to a primary nav tab — a deliberate signal that this is a core feature, not a buried setting. Household setup was moved into onboarding (not settings) because dietary conflict detection is useless without seeding members early.

Phase 3 — UI Design

8 screens designed, design system built

Designed all 8 MVP screens simultaneously in light and dark mode, powered by a shared design system (our-table-design-system.js). Every token, component, and spacing unit lives in one file — imported by all 8 screens. Palette, typography, and component decisions documented below.

Phase 3 — Prototype

Unified interactive prototype built

All 8 screens merged into a single interactive HTML prototype with real cross-screen navigation. Every major tap target wired up. Nav bar functional across all screens. Reviewable in any browser without a build step.

04 — Design Choices

Palette, typography, and visual language

The central design challenge: make a kitchen utility app feel warm and personal without defaulting to the terracotta/sage clichés that saturate every food app in the App Store.

Chosen Palette — Midnight Navy + Warm Amber

Navy reads as premium and distinct in a category dominated by warm terracottas. Amber provides the kitchen warmth without the overused rust/orange palette. Together they create a "golden kitchen at night" feeling — cosy but composed.

Rejected: Deep Plum + Warm Peach (#5C2B82 / #E8906A)

Seriously considered. Too editorial — reads as a beauty or lifestyle brand, not a kitchen utility. Saved for a future project where that editorial weight is an asset rather than a liability. The combination was beautiful but tonally wrong for an app centred on daily household cooking.

Typography

Wordmark & section headers: Georgia / Playfair Display (serif). Warmth and permanence — this is a household staple, not a tech utility. Serif signals tradition, family, recipes passed down.

UI & body text: -apple-system / Segoe UI (system sans). Maximum legibility in cook mode when hands are messy. Familiar and fast to read.

Scale: 22px section headers, 15–16px body, 12–13px metadata, 10–11px labels. All sizes meet WCAG AA minimum contrast ratios against their backgrounds.

Touch targets: Minimum 44px (Apple HIG). All interactive controls — including the +/− servings buttons on the recipe detail screen — were raised from 32px to 44px during the critique pass.

Key Visual Language Decisions
Decision

Recipe cards use brand colours, not food photos

Placeholder colours for recipe cards strictly use the navy/amber palette — no food-realistic greens or terracottas. Maintains brand coherence in the prototype and looks intentional rather than placeholder-like.

Decision

Nav bar elevated across all screens

The Fridge tab sits at position 3 in the nav bar — centre tab. Deliberately elevated above typical settings-buried placement to signal it as a primary feature, not an add-on.

Decision

Both themes designed simultaneously

Light and dark mode were designed in parallel, not added as an afterthought. Every component works in both. Signals craft in the portfolio. Token system makes this maintainable at scale.

06 — Version 1 Scope

What ships on launch day

Not every screen ships on day one. Some features need a seeded user base or backend infrastructure before they deliver value. V1 is focused on the core loop: save → cook → shop.

The V1 core loop

A new user signs up, sets up their household, imports a recipe from a TikTok link, sees it on their home feed, cooks it that evening by tapping through cook mode, and adds missing ingredients to the shared grocery list before their next shop. That loop must be flawless on day one.

Component Library

The design system exports a complete component set used across all 8 screens:

OT.c.btn()

Buttons

Primary, secondary, ghost variants. All 44px minimum height. Radius, weight, and colour automatically derived from the active theme token.

OT.c.navBar()

Navigation Bar

5-tab bottom nav. Active state with navy fill and amber indicator dot. Safe-area aware. Used identically across all 8 screens via single import.

OT.c.recipeCard()

Recipe Cards

Compact and full variants. Hero colour, title, time, source badge, and save action. Dietary conflict badges auto-rendered based on household member flags.

OT.c.avatar()

Member Avatars

Circular initials avatars. Each household member gets a unique palette colour (Will: navy, Emma: amber, Lily: blue). Used in the household row, profile, and recipe book.

OT.c.progressBar()

Progress Bars

Used in grocery list (shopping completion) and onboarding. Amber fill on blue-chip track. Height and radius configurable.

OT.c.settingsRow()

Settings Rows

Emoji + label + value + chevron row pattern. Used across Profile, Settings, Notifications, and Subscription screens. Consistent tap target height.
